WELCOME !! ~ ABOUT

When you hear the word entrepreneurship, what is the first the thing that comes to mind? For me it's the business reality TV show ‘The Apprentice’, consisting of Alan Sugar's cutthroat rebuttals, the highly competitive atmosphere and the many tears shed from the participants who have had their business dreams crushed. So when it came to navigating the topic of self-employment, that was the lens I saw it through: intimidating.

Is that cut-throat view the same idea that you have about entrepreneurship? Do you have an idea in your head that you have to first take a massive loan out of the bank or does the responsibility of being a sole trader put you off?

When it comes to flexibility, in terms of seeking alternative avenues of income other than the conventional route, it is safe to say that the options are few and far between. The majority of people feel more comfortable or feel that it is more convenient to work for a business under a boss. So, what is it about the prospect of self-employment that puts some people off then?

The establishment ‘Under30CEO’- the brainchild of YPA Marketing Center (YPA MC)- is a digital marketing agency exclusively dedicated in providing support at a mentor level to entrepreneurs. They have compiled a list of 14 misconceptions that people have about entrepreneurship.

Some of the misperceived notions that showed up on that list were: you need to be niche business, that when starting a business being in debt is absolutely necessary and that you’ve got to risk it all. One of the testimonials from Allie Siarto, the founder of Loudpixel a professional wedding and profile photographer said this about the financial misconception of entrepreneurship:

“I’m tired of seeing entrepreneurs preach that you’re not passionate about your idea unless you’re willing to give up everything for it. Plenty of people start a successful business without ever going into debt. We’ve always run our business as a blend of consulting and software. The consulting made us profitable from day one, which has given us the resources and time to build out the software.” ~ Allie Siarto

Statistics from the Office of National Statistics show that the percentage of unemployment rates for graduates and A - level graduates are the same, which suggests that going to university and getting a degree doesn't increase your chances of getting a job despite being in higher education.

When there is already enough competition in the job sector, and your degree qualification is equivalent to that of an A-level student, this feature will help you to explore the other options of employment.

This feature will focus specifically on Black-owned businesses started by recent graduates who have decided to start up their own brands, businesses and companies instead of following the masses and working for an employer.

RHODA'S STORY ~

GRADUATE START-UPS

Rhoda Olu is the founder of the online pop-up store The Afrostylist. She graduated from the University of Sheffield in 2016 with a degree in Psychology.

While in her final year of university, she started The Afrostylist in 2013, on a whim, "while I was supposed to be studying for the final year" she says.

She sells a selection of self-designed Afrocentric pendants, earrings and accessories. The Afrostylist has a vast online following with multiple Youtube famous and Instagram personalities purchasing and promoting her merch.

Some of the most well known online celebs seen wearing her jewellery have been, Shalom Blac the famous burn survivor, who has a following of 477k followers on Instagram and 6.4 M views on one of her make-up transformation Youtube videos and Shantania Beckford, who has 276,396 subscribers and 178k Instagram followers.

MICHAEL'S STORY ~

THE STRUGGLES

This is Michael Akuagwu, he s a 22 year-old bio-engineering graduate. His business is MLM Visuals, it is a freelance graphic design business, Michael works with various clients creating digital media, graphic design and physical prints for them. Alongside that, Michael is a Youtuber as well, a lot of his graphic design/ video editing experiences stemmed from his Youtube channel that he started on 2nd May 2014. Some of the visuals on his channel are marketed under 'MLM VISUALS'.

Michael started MLM Visuals during his time in university, as a means to gain more exeperiance with graphic design, It started off with him doing a few projects for societies which lead him on to being requested to create digital flyers for the University of Sheffield enterprise school. His designs have lead him to create things for the Mayor of London. His designs have also been featured on Camden Street's digital billboard London bus stop.

But the road to sucess has not always been so smooth sailing

Question 1- Do you think you were prepared for the workplace after leaving the safe hub of 15 plus years in education? Does getting a 1st class or A* always guarantee you an easy passage in the job market?

'It not that simple at all.'' That was something I realised first within the first month of leaving university. I graduated with a 2:2, If I wanted to get into engineering I'm already starting at the bottom against people with higher degrees. What I find crazy is when you first get into an industry, they will either teach you new things or they will tell you the thing that you spend learning for three years at university, you actually don't need it for this job or it its just filler to get you into a position in a lot of cases. I found this really annoying because, you start to think what was the point of paying this much money at university every year and then at the end of it you're not even guaranteed a job. It makes no sense. It's sad that universities have not really grasped that. Nowadays experience is valued over qualifications with a rise in apprenticeships and schemes that allow people to get into the workforce a lot easier without worrying about having to get a 1st class Your education is great but, if you didn't do any extra-curricular activities now to a lot of employers you look like a robot because you've been working so much at your degree, but so has everybody else, other people have been able to do things on the side and learn certain transferable skills from that. So educational achievement is not the only option, education only teaches you one thing, but life and all of its experiences teaches you a wide array of other things. What you make out of your time a university and for yourself too helps you. hUndreds and thousands of people are applying for the same jobs, you need to find different ways to make you stand out. Some in an hiring team once told me that he has clones of the same set of people; can people stop saying the same thing in applications these conventional applications go straight into the bin. Nowadays a lot of companies are seeking people who have started things up themselves because it shows that they have initiative and drive and passion as well. .

Question 2 -Do you ever regret taking the entrepreneur route?

Not at all funnily enough. Ever since graduation, I've had loads of convsersations with my Mum, she has seen both the highs and the lows. I think a lot of people think that a entrepreneurship is synonymous with getting money but that is not always the case. People forget that it takes a lot of diligence, hard work and patience. You're not really working a 9-5 you're working an 8 to 11pm because you're are working for yourself.There is a lot of groundwork that needs to take place, in my case I had to sacrifice things like going out to see friends etcetera. Take a sick day at a normal job you get holiday/sick pay, if you do that as an entrepreneur, that's coming out your own pocket. I do not regret this route, it's been hard and there have been a lot of learning curves, this may be the most financially tight I've ever been, but it has the happiest that I've ever been."

Question 3 - Is it more financially rewarding or stable than not being an entrepreneur

I'll probably say yes, it depends, because at first when you start a business there are a lot of expenses in order for you to start. So you have to register your business, whether you want it limited or self-employed. There is a lot of money that goes into your initial startup, it might take you a while to attract a solid customer base. In my experiences because I'm a freelancer, it did take me a while to get customers and especially because i wasn't charging so much at first. At first in terms of money I was probably making a lot less than somebody who had a full-time job, even know sometimes I am when I do the hours and the math. But eventually the more you work at it and the more people know of you and you build that loyal customer base, eventually it can be more profitable than doing a graduate full-time job. Especially when you get to that level, as a video designer and editor you can charge hundreds of pounds for a couple hours of editing. In terms of hourly pay, someone who works for a company will usually get paid less.

Question 4 - What are some of the hardship that you struggled with

Not having any money.... not having any money for sure. There are times your working and you haven't got anything in and the money is supposed to come at the end of the project. So I remember when I had one of my first company jobs; this was for a magazine company and they needed content to be edited. This was something that would have taken a day or two to edit but because of a lack of communication it prolonged to a month long. I was waiting out for this money to come so I could pay for things, as it was coming towards Christmas too. But because the lack of communication as so poor it wasn't until January that I got paid. Also when things seemed a bit quiet, it did take a while to build a clientele and publicity for myself I would have questions like: what gonna happen, am I ever going to be able to get a job, will I get a chance to work with some people, how am I going to survive? Another hardship was dealing with indecisive clients changing their minds over designs, when you have to make revisions and changes things, it cuts into your hours If you have already set them a set price that set price might as well be you working for free, because you've spent too many more ridiculous hours, that could have been avoided if they had been more detailed with their proposals.

Though they are definately struggles that come with taking the entrepreneurial route, there are also benefits that come with it that a conventional 9-5 job can't provide.

WHY ENTREPRENEURSHIP

Why choose the entrepreneurship route

Leonie is the founder of the African print ties and apparel business Debonoire.

Social benefits – Envoys can network to improve their own social conditions as well as those of others. Working to create enabling environments and fight inequalities, their contributions and collective dedication can support health, housing, education, living standards and the social dimensions of all in the global society, whilst creating wealth and celebrating success.
Economic benefits – Envoys can use the network to share ideas with others, to promote their products or to attract funds. They can also identify organisations and individuals who can help or mentor them. But as with the social benefits, the economic benefits that can be gained by changing the enabling environment can have gained for all in the global society.
Environmental benefits – Envoys are ‘virtual’ and work in the most environmentally friendly way possible. But they also need to be present to promote issues direct to policymakers, education and the media. The green economy and climate change mitigation is being worked on fastest and most efficiently at the grassroots level and a serious dimension of the Envoys commitment is to support this sector as there is only one planet!
